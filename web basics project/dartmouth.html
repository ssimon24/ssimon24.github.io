dartmouth archives

My journey in the field of archives began at Rauner Special Collections Library at Dartmouth College. The summer of 2022, I was did archival research at the library through Dartmouth's Historical Accountability Student Research Program, which "offers research opportunities for Dartmouth undergraduates to explore primary sources in our collections related to issues of diversity and inclusion in Dartmouth history." As a queer student studying English literature, I wanted to look into Dartmouth's queer history through the lens of works of fiction published in campus literary magazine.

Historical Accountability Student Research Fellowship

  • Uncovered over 50 stories from Dartmouth’s literary magazines with queer themes by analyzing 40 years’ worth of material.
  • Considered the uncovered stories alongside College records that exposed nonnormative Dartmouth cultures – i.e. the recordsof President Hopkins, student theses, and the letters of Clifford Orr, class of 1922 – to gain a clearer image of student life.
  • Developed an interactive website and public presentation to promote community knowledge of Dartmouth queer history.

After this research project, I knew I could not go back to the life I had before working in an archive, so I began working as a student assistant in the library. I worked with Jay Satterfield, Head of Rare Books and Special Collections.

Assistant to the Head of Rare Books and Special Collections

  • Interacted with and handled rare books and manuscripts throughout the collection, spanning centuries of materials, throughindependent research, the paging of materials, and collaborations with the Head of Special Collections.
  • Entrusted with long-term collection management project to identify books in the collection that were not visible in the catalog.
  • Spearheaded the design of class lessons and exhibits that utilized the collections to encourage student interaction and curiosity.

During my senior year, I also took various classes that involved work at Rauner Library. These classes included History of the Book, a critical museum studies class, and two others that culminated in digitial exhibits. You can find the exhibits for these two classes below.

Dartmouth Black Lives Oral History Project

  • Interviewed Reverend Warner Traynham – one of three Black students to graduate from Dartmouth in 1957 and the Dean ofthe College’s center for religious life in the 70s – about his life, and curated a digital exhibit based on the oral history.
  • Incorporated original archival research and Traynham’s personal stories into the exhibit to highlight his experiences as one ofDartmouth’s first Black graduates, his interactions with campus administrators, and his impact on queer student life in the 70s.

A Tale of Three Administrations: Rev. Warner Traynham’s Experience as both Student and Dean

Reading Artifacts: The Material Culture of Science

  • Created a digital exhibit inspired by a letter I found while working as student assistant at the library. The letter was found on the body of a dead confederate soldier during the Civil War, and it was stained by the soldier's own blood.
  • The exhibit considers the various ways that this letter exists as a complicated object. It asks questions about the letter's history and context, and it also considers the ethics around its existence in the collection, its use, and its display.

"For Preservation": The Civil War, Blood Stains, and Collection Ethics
